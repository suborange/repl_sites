WHAT IS QUANTUM COMPUTING?

A quantum computer uses a qubit, or quantum bit, to compute data. A qubit can physically be a photon, nucleus, or even many other possibilites, like an electron. Some researchers have used the outer electron of phosphorus to act as a qubit. All electrons abide by electromagnetic laws, and will align with a magnetic field being the lowest energy (0) state, or vice versa, becoming the highest energy (1) state. This figure shows IBM's giant quantum computer, being mostly a cooling system to keep the small quantum chip at temperatures close to 0 kelvin.

Using qubits opposed to classical bits, or binary digits, has many advantages, mostly outside the realm of practicality. A classical computer can be really fast and process real, more practical data using bits, but struggles when attempting enormous, exponentially large computations or algorithms. This is where the quantum computer excels, handling exponentially gigantic computations, and where quantum engineers are developing towards.

FUN FACTS

With IBM's system, it can get to a temperature of 15 millikelvin, so very close to 0 kelvin, or absolute zero. This is the coolest thing that exists in the universe, even much cooler than space. (As cold as a black hole? Interesting thought to follow up on).

This system has 433 qubits, and relating to the exponential nature of qubits, an N number of qubits equates to 2N of classic bits of information, meaning 2433 bits. Now that is astronomical! The total amount of particles in the universe is around 2300, so this quantum computer contains more information than all of our currently known universe!!

Quantum Computers will continue to improve, increasing the number of qubits exponentially(heh) and increasing the computational power drastically to solve some of the hardest, maybe even impossible problems we are unable to solve today with all other modern technology.
