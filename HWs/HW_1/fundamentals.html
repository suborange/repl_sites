FUNDAMENTALS

I do my best to translate what I researched into my own words, so there may be some inaccuracies or (dare I say) misinformation.

Keeping the most basic explaination in mind, Quantum Computing seems rather simple and similar to a classical computer, where bits have a result of 0 and 1, and qubits having a result of 0 and 1. However, this is where the magic of quantum physics exists, and a qubit can actually exist in both states at the same time, or even any state in between simultaneously. Only when the qubit gets measured would the result "collapse" to be either the result we see of 0 or 1. There becomes a relative probability of which state the qubit will be when eventually measured. Qubits exhibit this magic behavior making a quantum computer have incredible potential.

COMPUTATIONAL ADVANTAGE

A qubit has the ability to be in both states at the same time. This spectrum of infinite states is referred to as the quantum superposition, more details under Q~Computing page. Before any measuring is completed, there is a relative probability, some percentage, of finding the qubit to be one, or the other, of the base states. One qubit has 2 possible states, and therefore 2 quantum superpositions. Now combining 2 interacting qubits, it grows to have 4 possible states between the 2 qubits. But wait, again this sounds exactly like classical bits, where 2 bits also have 4 possible states - 00, 01, 10, and 11. To get the result using classical bits, 2 bits of information are needed to determine said result, the first digit, like a 0, then the second digit, like a 1, combining to the result of 10 (goes from right to left), or 2 in decimal.


Now with 2 qubits having 4 possible states, this also means there would be 4 total superpositions. Per the figure - the α(alpha) coefficent times the 00, plus the β(beta) coefficient times the 01, plus the γ(gamma) coefficient times the 10, and finally plus the δ(delta) coefficient times the 11, equates to the 4 possible states of these 2 qubits. Conclusively, 2 bits result in 2 pieces of information, the 2 bits; 2 qubits result in 4 pieces of information, the 4 possible superpositions. This will continue to grow, where 3 qubits would have 8 superpositions. Therefore, an N number of qubits equates to 2N in relation to classical bit information.

MISCALLANEOUS

Additionally, quantum entaglement is important for qubits to work. Finally, it is not possible for us to measure the superposition states, thus all this previous possible data is lost as soon as it does get measured. Due to this, relative probability and quantum logical gates now come into play. A quantum computer is designed, so that the logic operations can allow for the qubits to have deterministic results, meaning to get to the final result that can actually be measured in one unique state. A superposition is further explained under superposition section in the Q~computing page.

Quantum Computers are not a replacement for classical computers, as again classical computers are insanely fast, thus are good for calculators, 3D processing, audio, and more. A quantum computer does computations differently, and in many cases is slower than classical, but the differences are highly advantageous in areas like encryption, algorithms and more that are actually slow, or even impossible for classical computers to solve (at least within the limited time of human existance).
